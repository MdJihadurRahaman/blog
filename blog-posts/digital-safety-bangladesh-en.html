Protecting Young People Online: Understanding the Problems and Finding Real Solutions

Introduction: Bangladesh's young generation stands at a crossroads. On one side, there's unlimited potential in the digital world. On the other, new and evolving safety threats. From cyberbullying to deepfake technology abuse, the challenges keep getting more complex. This report identifies the main digital safety problems facing young Bangladeshis and outlines practical, community-based solutions that can actually make a difference.

Major Digital Safety Problems in Bangladesh

1. Cyberbullying and Online Harassment

This is currently the biggest concern for young people. Online harassment and psychological abuse are having serious impacts on their lives.

ğŸ“Š Statistics: 85% of university students in Bangladesh consider cyberbullying a serious problem

Main Types of Cyberbullying:

  • Body Shaming: Making fun of someone's physical appearance, which is the most common form of bullying
  • Online Abuse and Trolling: Aggressive comments on social media platforms
  • Defamatory Content: Sharing photos or information to damage someone's reputation

2. Technology-Facilitated Gender-Based Violence (TFGBV)

Women face significantly more danger online and are targeted far more frequently. Gender-based violence on digital platforms has become a critical issue.

ğŸ“Š Statistics: Around 64% of women aged 15 to 35 experience some form of online violence

Main Complaints Received at Police Cyber Support Center for Women:

  • 41% Doxing: Publishing private information without permission
  • 18% Account Hacking: Taking over Facebook or other social media accounts
  • 17% Blackmail: Threatening with private photos or information
  • 9% Impersonation: Creating fake IDs to spread misinformation

3. Online Sexual Exploitation of Children

Children are in an extremely vulnerable position on digital platforms. Online grooming and sexual harassment are increasing at alarming rates.

ğŸ“Š Statistics: Approximately 23% of children in Bangladesh face severe risks on online platforms

  • Highest Risk on Facebook: 77% of children face risks on Facebook
  • Online Grooming: Adults gain trust and then exploit children
  • Unwanted Sexual Messages: Sending inappropriate content to children

4. Deepfake and AI Technology Abuse

The misuse of artificial intelligence to create fake videos and images using women's or acquaintances' faces has become a major threat.

  • Pornographic Deepfakes: Creating objectionable content using women's faces
  • Blackmail: Extorting money by threatening with deepfakes
  • Social Defamation: Damaging someone's reputation with fake videos

5. Misinformation and Propaganda

Spreading rumors or false information to create social unrest and harass individuals is a big problem.

  • Social Harassment: Using false information to humiliate someone socially
  • Political Instigation: Creating political instability with misleading information
  • Religious Hatred: Creating communal tension with fake news

6. Lack of Digital Literacy

Many young people are not aware of basic digital security, which makes them easy targets for criminals.

  • Weak Passwords: Using easily guessable passwords
  • Phishing Attacks: Losing information by clicking on fake links or emails
  • Online Scams: Getting deceived by fraudulent offers or lotteries
  • Unknown Reporting Procedures: Not knowing where to report when crimes occur

Mental and Social Impact

Impact on Mental Health

These digital safety problems have profound negative effects on young people's mental health.

ğŸ“Š Statistics: Young women who experience cyberbullying have four times higher rates of severe depression compared to others

  • Depression and Anxiety: Mental stress increases due to online harassment
  • Loss of Confidence: Continuous bullying reduces self-esteem
  • Social Isolation: Fear of harassment leads to reduced social contact

Self-Censorship and Moral Policing

Due to family and social conservatism, girls are always under surveillance online.

ğŸ“Š Statistics: 71.5% of young women feel unsafe expressing their opinions online

  • Reduced Freedom of Expression: Hiding opinions due to fear of harassment
  • Social Surveillance: Pressure from family and society
  • Hindered Creativity: Unable to express their true opinions

LGBTQIA+ Community

Young people from this community face extensive hate speech and propaganda online.

  • Pressure to Hide Identity: Forced to conceal their identity due to societal fear
  • Hate Speech: Facing hateful comments and threats
  • Social Isolation: Discrimination both online and offline

Community-Level Digital Safety Solutions

Creating awareness about digital safety at the community level requires more than just legal strictness. It needs an integrated approach combining education, entertainment, and direct participation. Here are practical and creative solutions:

1. Role of Educational Institutions and Teachers

Including in School Curriculum:

It's essential to include digital ethics, online safety, and responsible digital behavior in school curricula. Students will learn digital citizenship from an early age.

Teacher Training (TOT):

  • Providing special training to teachers so they can identify and address cyber risks
  • Creating online portals where teachers can exchange experiences
  • Building resource libraries for teachers

2. Sensitizing Parents

Parental Sensitization:

Organizing special awareness meetings for parents is extremely important. Since many children in Bangladesh use the internet on shared devices with family members, parents must understand online risks.

Open Discussion:

  • Building a culture of open conversation about digital safety in families
  • Discussing internet usage rules with children
  • Creating guidebooks and video tutorials for parents

3. Creative and Entertainment-Based Methods

Pop Culture and Mascots:

Running campaigns using popular cartoon mascots (like Meena) to quickly raise children's awareness. This helps children learn easily and remember better.

Entertainment Content:

  • Educational Puppet Shows: Teaching digital safety through puppet performances
  • Comic Strips: Cyber safety stories in simple language with pictures
  • Web Series: Attractive digital safety-based dramas for young people

Game Shows and Competitions:

  • Organizing game shows about online safety
  • Debate competitions and essay writing contests
  • Digital safety rallies and awareness programs

4. Youth Leadership and Peer Education

Digital Literacy Ambassadors:

Creating digital literacy ambassadors from among young people, who will spread awareness among their peers. Young people easily trust and accept what their contemporaries say.

Digital Citizenship Charter:

  • Creating a Digital Citizenship Charter with direct student participation
  • Commitment to protecting personal information and maintaining online etiquette
  • Launching peer-to-peer mentoring programs

5. Community Outreach and Marginalized Group Participation

Local-Level Meetings:

Organizing community gatherings, fairs, and workshops to increase awareness in rural and marginalized areas. Involving local leaders and religious leaders.

Inclusive Campaigns:

  • Specialized digital literacy programs for children with disabilities
  • Awareness content in local languages for marginalized communities
  • Visual content for illiterate and less-educated parents

6. Survivor Voices and Support Systems

Sharing Survivor Experiences:

Highlighting the experiences or voices of those who have been victims of online violence so others can be cautious and social resistance against crime can be built.

Emergency Helplines:

Report digital crimes or harassment:

National Helpline: 1098

Emergency Service: 109 (Prevention of Violence Against Women and Children)

Support System:

  • Simplifying cyber crime reporting procedures
  • Providing mental health counseling services for victims
  • Ensuring legal assistance and counseling services

ğŸ“ Conclusion

Ensuring digital safety in Bangladesh requires more than just technical solutions or legal measures. We need an integrated and multifaceted approach where educational institutions, families, communities, and young people themselves play active roles.

Remember: Digital safety is not just a technical issue. It's a question of human rights, mental health, and social equality. Every young person has the right to be safe online, and protecting that right is everyone's responsibility.
